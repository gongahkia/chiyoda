\begin{abstract}
Emergency evacuation is usually modeled as a physical routing problem, with
information appearing as a fixed scenario assumption: agents either know exits,
observe hazards, hear a warning, or do not. We present \emph{Chiyoda}, an
Information-Theoretic Evacuation Dynamics framework that treats emergency
communication as a controllable intervention in a coupled physical-information
system. Agents carry probabilistic beliefs over exits and hazards, exchange
distorted information through local gossip, receive beacon and responder
messages, suffer physiological impairment under CBRN-like hazards, and choose
routes through belief-weighted path planning under social-force crowd
dynamics. Chiyoda compares static, global, responder-relay, entropy-targeted,
density-aware, exposure-aware, and bottleneck-avoidance policies using
telemetry that links belief entropy, belief accuracy, exposure, queueing, exit
imbalance, and evacuation time. In a 50-seed primary study with
\statRunCount{} runs and \statInterventionEvents{} intervention events,
\statBestPolicy{} achieves the highest information-safety efficiency
(\statBestEfficiency{}), while global and adaptive policies reveal that broad
reach and entropy reduction are not sufficient safety objectives. The central
claim is not that more information is always better, but that emergency
communication has measurable safety tradeoffs: rapid uncertainty reduction can
improve local decision-making while also synchronizing crowds into harmful
convergence when timing, credibility, or spatial targeting are wrong.
\end{abstract}
