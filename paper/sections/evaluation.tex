\section{Evaluation}
\label{sec:evaluation}

We evaluate Chiyoda on \texttt{study\_information\_control.yaml}, a
five-seed comparison of eight emergency communication regimes: no deliberate
intervention, static beacon broadcast, global PA broadcast, responder relay,
entropy-targeted communication, density-aware communication, exposure-aware
communication, and bottleneck-avoidance communication. The exported bundle
contains \statRunCount{} runs, \statVariantCount{} variant aggregates, and
\statInterventionEvents{} intervention events. Each run uses the same
station-sarin scenario, a 120-agent commuter/tourist population, asymmetric
initial information, gossip, beacon infrastructure, three delayed responders,
and a 500-step horizon.

\subsection{Outcome Measures}
\label{subsec:outcomes}

We report three classes of outcome. Physical outcomes include evacuated agents,
mean travel time, incapacitation count, mean hazard exposure, peak bottleneck
queue, and exit imbalance. Information outcomes include mean entropy, entropy
reduction, intervention reach, entropy reduction attributable to interventions,
and belief-accuracy gain. Coupled outcomes include information-safety
efficiency and harmful convergence. These coupled metrics are the main object
of the paper: they ask whether a policy buys useful belief improvement without
creating exposure or queueing pressure.

\subsection{Aggregate Findings}
\label{subsec:aggregate-findings}

Table~\ref{tab:aggregate} summarizes the variant aggregates. The first result
is negative but important: no intervention policy dominates the baseline across
physical evacuation outcomes at this seed count. The no-intervention baseline
evacuates 11.8 agents on average, while static beacons evacuate 12.4, responder
relay evacuates 11.4, and the other adaptive policies evacuate between 9.4 and
10.6. Mann--Whitney tests against the baseline do not show significant
improvement in evacuated count, travel time, incapacitation, or exposure for
any policy at $n=5$ seeds. This is exactly why the paper cannot simply claim
``more information improves evacuation.''

The second result is that communication policy matters even when physical
outcomes remain noisy. Static beacons have the highest information-safety
efficiency in this study, \statBestEfficiency{}, and are the current best
policy by that metric (\statBestPolicy{}). They produce a modest evacuation
increase over baseline (+0.6 agents), a small travel-time reduction (-0.29s),
and lower peak queue (2.0 vs.\ 2.2), but the effect is not statistically
decisive. Their strength is efficiency: local, repeated, high-credibility
messages produce belief improvement without the full synchronization cost of
global broadcast.

The third result is that wide reach is not the same as useful reach. Global
broadcast reaches 1823.6 recipients on average across intervention events, far
more than static beacons (492.6), but its information-safety efficiency is only
0.0057. It also has the lowest entropy reduction among intervention policies
(0.044 aggregate entropy reduction versus 0.195 in the baseline trajectory).
The model interpretation is that a broad message can homogenize beliefs without
necessarily producing safer route allocations.

The fourth result is the clearest support for the paper's thesis:
entropy-targeted communication improves belief accuracy strongly but can
backfire at the system level. It produces the largest intervention accuracy
gain (1.713), but also the highest peak bottleneck queue (3.6), the fewest
evacuated agents (9.4), and the highest harmful-convergence index (8.62). The
Mann--Whitney comparison against no intervention finds the queue increase
borderline at $p=0.048$ with a large effect size ($d=1.57$). This is not a
final causal proof, but it is a useful warning: reducing the uncertainty of
the most uncertain agents can synchronize newly informed agents into the same
capacity-constrained routes.

\subsection{Hypotheses Revisited}
\label{subsec:hypotheses-results}

H1 predicted that adaptive targeting would improve information-safety
efficiency over global broadcast. The result is mixed. Entropy-targeted,
density-aware, exposure-aware, and bottleneck-avoidance policies all have
nonzero efficiency, but only bottleneck avoidance exceeds global broadcast by a
substantial margin; entropy targeting and density targeting improve some
information measures while increasing harmful convergence.

H2 predicted that global broadcast would reduce entropy quickly but risk route
synchronization. The data support the second half more than the first. Global
broadcast has high reach and high harmful convergence, but its aggregate
entropy reduction is low. In this model, broadcast reach is partly wasted when
messages arrive after agents have already converged or when congestion
information is insufficiently differentiated.

H3 predicted that exposure-aware targeting would reduce hazard exposure.
Exposure-aware policy has the lowest mean hazard exposure among intervention
policies (3.140), slightly below baseline (3.147), but the difference is small
and not significant. The result suggests exposure targeting is directionally
reasonable but underpowered in the current scenario.

H4 predicted that responder relay would be robust but delayed. This is the
strongest supported hypothesis: responder relay has the lowest mean hazard
exposure among the high-efficiency policies (3.050), the lowest harmful
convergence among intervention policies except exposure-aware targeting, and
the second-best information-safety efficiency. Its limitation is timing:
responders enter after the evacuation process has already begun.

\subsection{Interpretation}
\label{subsec:interpretation}

The study supports the paper's central framing rather than a single winning
policy. Information interventions should be evaluated as safety-control
actions, not as monotonic entropy minimizers. A policy can reach many agents
and still have low safety efficiency; another can improve belief accuracy and
still worsen bottleneck pressure. The promising direction is therefore not
``broadcast more,'' but ``broadcast locally, credibly, and with awareness of
capacity and exposure.'' Static beacon and responder relay policies currently
look safest under this scenario; entropy targeting is scientifically
interesting precisely because it exposes a failure mode.

\subsection{Threats to Evaluation}
\label{subsec:evaluation-threats}

These results are preliminary. Five seeds are enough to reveal qualitative
tradeoffs and obvious failure modes, but not enough for final PhD-level claims
about policy superiority. The next study should increase seed count, vary
hazard severity and population familiarity, and add calibrated geometries. The
current metrics also depend on the chosen harmful-convergence formula; future
work should test alternative normalizations and compare them against
trajectory-level bottleneck events.
