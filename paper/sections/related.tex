\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

Chiyoda sits between pedestrian dynamics, hazard-coupled evacuation, and
information-aware decision models. Social-force models represent pedestrians
as self-driven particles whose movement emerges from goal attraction,
repulsion, and contact forces~\cite{helbing1995social}. Cellular automata
models instead discretize space and update movement through local transition
rules~\cite{burstedde2001simulation}. Both traditions are useful for evacuation
because physical failure is often a capacity problem: exit flow, bottlenecks,
counterflow, and density-dependent slowdown can dominate individual intent.
Chiyoda uses this physical tradition as the substrate, but the paper's novelty
does not rest on a new crowd-force law.

Hazard-coupled evacuation models add a time-varying environment. Toxic-gas and
gaseous-material simulations show that contaminant spread, perception, and
information transmission can change route choice and evacuation
outcomes~\cite{makmul2020cellular,liu2021toxicgas}. This line of work is
closest to Chiyoda's CBRN-like setting. The difference is experimental focus:
prior models often include information perception as a behavioral factor,
whereas Chiyoda makes emergency communication itself the object of controlled
comparison. Policies are not merely scenario settings; they are interventions
that can be timed, targeted, budgeted, and evaluated.

Incomplete-information evacuation models provide the decision-theoretic
counterpart. Bayesian and game-theoretic formulations study route choice under
uncertainty and congestion~\cite{wang2023bayesian}, while social-group ABM
surveys emphasize communication, affiliation, and collective behavior as
central modeling concerns~\cite{templeton2023social,senanayake2024agent}.
Chiyoda builds on this direction by making uncertainty observable as telemetry.
Belief entropy, belief accuracy, intervention reach, exposure, queueing, and
exit imbalance are exported together, which lets the paper ask whether a
belief improvement is also a safety improvement.

The resulting distinction is methodological. Chiyoda treats communication as
an experimental control surface rather than a binary assumption about whether
agents know enough. It asks how an emergency system should allocate limited
communication budget under uncertainty, and it measures the side effects of
that allocation. This makes message timing, targeting, credibility, and
spatial reach comparable interventions with measurable safety tradeoffs. The
contribution is therefore not a claim that Chiyoda is more realistic than all
prior evacuation models; it is a claim that information interventions can and
should be evaluated as safety-control actions.
